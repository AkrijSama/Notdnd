# Chapter 7 — Progression

*The Inkborne System — Core Book*

You get stronger in Inkborne the same way you get anything in Inkborne: by
making things true in the world. Experience is not a mood, a reward for good
roleplay, or something a generous narrator hands out. It is a ledger.

---

## The ledger law

**XP flows only from committed events.** When the world's record changes —
and only then — experience pays out:

- **Quest stages** — each stage of a quest you complete, and the quest itself;
- **Thread resolutions** — a story pressure you saw through to its end,
  however it ended;
- **Discoveries** — places found, secrets uncovered, truths dragged into
  the light;
- **Combat victories** — fights won, measured by what you actually beat.

Notice what's not on the list: *narration*. A stirring speech, a clever
turn of phrase, a scene the table loved — these are why you play, but they
are not events, and events are the only thing the ledger pays. **You cannot
talk the GM into levels**, because the GM doesn't award XP at all. The
world's ledger does, automatically, the moment an event commits. If it
isn't in the record, it didn't pay.

This is the same referee principle from Chapter 1 wearing its progression
hat: the narrator has the voice, the engine has the books.

## The curve **[PROPOSED]**

Advancing from your current level to the next costs:

> **XP to next level = current level × 100**

| Level | Cost of next level | Total XP from 1 |
|------:|------:|------:|
| 1 → 2 | 100 | 100 |
| 4 → 5 | 400 | 1,000 |
| 9 → 10 | 900 | 4,500 |
| 14 → 15 | 1,400 | 10,500 |
| 19 → 20 | 1,900 | 19,000 |

For scale, typical committed events pay on the order of: minor discovery
**10–25**, quest stage **25–50**, significant combat victory **25–100** (by
threat), full quest or thread resolution **100–250** — exact values are
world-book content, validated against these bands. At that rate a new
character levels in a session or two, while the climb from 19 to 20 is a
saga: roughly ten resolved threads' worth of consequence. Early levels teach
the game fast; late levels are earned in stories.

> *Why linear-cost (quadratic total)? Steep enough that high levels feel
> like eras, shallow enough that a mid-level character joining a fresh world
> book isn't decades behind. Exponential curves make the cap mythical;
> flat curves make it routine. Linear splits the difference and the math
> stays legible at the table.*

## What a level is

Leveling is a **recompute, not a shopping trip**. When you level:

1. **Stats grow by your class's growth-profile.** Every class carries a
   growth-profile: a level-indexed array (data in the world book, not code)
   declaring which stat rises at which level. Profiles are
   **budget-bounded and engine-validated** — every class spends the same
   total stat budget across 20 levels **[PROPOSED: +10 stat points total,
   no stat raised above 20]**; where and when it spends them is the class's
   identity. A bruiser front-loads STR and VIT; a wire-ghost drips DEX and
   INT. The validator rejects any profile that overspends, so no world book
   can print a broken class.
2. **Everything derived recomputes.** HP (from VIT and level), Attack,
   Evasion, Accuracy, turn speed, crit — all downstream numbers refresh
   automatically from the new stats. You never do this math; the sheet is
   always the truth of the current stats.
3. **Class features arrive on schedule.** Each class's world book declares
   which levels grant which features. The core system defines only the
   slot: *a class may grant features at levels of its choosing, within its
   tier* (below).
4. **Every 5th level, a feat.** At levels **5, 10, 15, and 20** you pick a
   feat **[PROPOSED — N=5]**. Feats are drawn from the world book's list
   (plus the one your race gave you at creation, per the race contract).
   *Why 5: it lands the feat on each tier boundary — a feat is your tier's
   graduation, the thing you carry into the next band. Four picks plus the
   racial feat gives five across a career: each one a real identity
   decision, none of them filler.*

## The cap and the tiers

**Level 20 is the cap.** Beyond it, growth is horizontal — gear, feats
retrained, standing in the world — never vertical.

The twenty levels split into four **tier bands**, and world books gate
content against them:

| Tier | Levels | What it means in play |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | 1–5 | Local. Your problems fit in a district, a village, a job. |
| **II** | 6–10 | Regional. Factions know your name; your problems have maps. |
| **III** | 11–15 | Pivotal. You bend the setting's big conflicts; they bend back. |
| **IV** | 16–20 | Defining. The world book's endgame — its towers, thrones, and final doors. |

A world book may lock anything to a tier — a district you can't survive, a
power that won't take a Tier-I student, a door that reads the ledger before
it opens. Tiers are the world's honest way of saying *not yet*, and the
ledger is the only way to change its mind.
