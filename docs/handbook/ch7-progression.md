# Chapter 7 — Progression

*The Inkborne System — Core Book*

You get stronger in Inkborne the same way you get anything in Inkborne: by
making things true in the world. Experience is not a mood, a reward for good
roleplay, or something a generous narrator hands out. It is a ledger.

---

## The ledger law

**XP flows only from committed events.** When the world's record changes —
and only then — experience pays out:

- **Quest stages** — each stage of a quest you complete, and the quest itself;
- **Thread resolutions** — a story pressure you saw through to its end,
  however it ended;
- **Discoveries** — places found, secrets uncovered, truths dragged into
  the light;
- **Combat victories** — fights won, measured by what you actually beat.

Notice what's not on the list: *narration*. A stirring speech, a clever
turn of phrase, a scene the table loved — these are why you play, but they
are not events, and events are the only thing the ledger pays. **You cannot
talk the GM into levels**, because the GM doesn't award XP at all. The
world's ledger does, automatically, the moment an event commits. If it
isn't in the record, it didn't pay.

This is the same referee principle from Chapter 1 wearing its progression
hat: the narrator has the voice, the engine has the books.

## Milestones and levels — two words, two jobs

The chassis measures growth in **milestones**: roughly twenty mechanical
waypoints where your character genuinely changes. Your **world book**
measures growth in **levels**: the number on your sheet, scaled however the
world's fiction demands — a lean world may show twenty, a tower world may
show two hundred and fifty. The world book maps its milestones onto
**breakthrough levels** of its choosing; every level between breakthroughs
is a **minor level** — a small HP tick and the world's flavor of progress
(a rank, a stripe, a louder reputation), never a stat point, feat, or
feature. The number on the sheet is the world talking. The milestones
underneath are the engine, and the engine's books always balance.

> *Why split them? Because the cap was math, not the number. What the
> chassis must bound is the growth budget — how much mechanical character
> you can ever buy. What the world must own is the fiction of the climb —
> and some fictions need level 250 the way others need level 20. Minor
> levels keep the drumbeat of progress; milestones keep the math honest.*

## The curve

Advancing from your current milestone to the next costs:

> **XP to next milestone = current milestone × 100**

| Milestone | Cost of next | Total XP from 1 |
|------:|------:|------:|
| 1 → 2 | 100 | 100 |
| 4 → 5 | 400 | 1,000 |
| 9 → 10 | 900 | 4,500 |
| 14 → 15 | 1,400 | 10,500 |
| 19 → 20 | 1,900 | 19,000 |

For scale, typical committed events pay on the order of: minor discovery
**10–25**, quest stage **25–50**, significant combat victory **25–100** (by
threat), full quest or thread resolution **100–250** — exact values are
world-book content, validated against these bands. At that rate a new
character reaches a milestone in a session or two, while the climb from the
19th to the 20th is a saga: roughly ten resolved threads' worth of
consequence. Early milestones teach the game fast; late ones are earned in
stories. A world book divides each milestone's XP span across its minor
levels however it likes — the drip is display; the curve above is law.

> *Why linear-cost (quadratic total)? Steep enough that late milestones feel
> like eras, shallow enough that a mid-career character joining a fresh world
> book isn't decades behind. Exponential curves make the summit mythical;
> flat curves make it routine. Linear splits the difference and the math
> stays legible at the table.*

## What a milestone is

A milestone is a **recompute, not a shopping trip**. When you reach one:

1. **Stats grow by your class's growth-profile.** Every class carries a
   growth-profile: a milestone-indexed array (data in the world book, not
   code) declaring which stat rises at which milestone. Profiles are
   **budget-bounded and engine-validated** — every class spends the same
   total stat budget across the ~20 milestones — **+10 stat points total,
   no stat raised above 20** — where and when it spends them is the class's
   identity. A bruiser front-loads STR and VIT; a wire-ghost drips DEX and
   INT. The validator rejects any profile that overspends, so no world book
   can print a broken class.
2. **Everything derived recomputes.** HP (from VIT and milestone), Attack,
   Evasion, Accuracy, turn speed, crit — all downstream numbers refresh
   automatically from the new stats. You never do this math; the sheet is
   always the truth of the current stats.
3. **Class features arrive on schedule.** Each class's world book declares
   which milestones grant which features. The core system defines only the
   slot: *a class may grant features at milestones of its choosing, within
   its tier* (below).
4. **Every 5th milestone, a feat.** At milestones **5, 10, 15, and 20** you
   pick a feat. Feats are drawn from the world book's list
   (plus the one your race gave you at creation, per the race contract).
   *Why 5: it lands the feat on each tier graduation — a feat is your
   tier's diploma, the thing you carry into the next band. Four picks plus
   the racial feat gives five across a career: each one a real identity
   decision, none of them filler.*

A **minor level** is none of the above: a small HP tick (the world book
sets it, the validator bounds it) and whatever fiction the world attaches.
It never grants stats, feats, or features — those live at milestones only.

## The budget and the tiers

**The chassis caps the budget, not the number on your sheet.** Roughly
twenty milestones spend the whole growth budget; past the last one, growth
is horizontal — gear, feats retrained, standing in the world — never
vertical. What *displayed* level that final milestone wears is the world
book's business: 20 in a lean world, 250 at the top of a tower. If a world
caps its characters' levels, that cap is **world lore** — a law of its
fiction, not a law of the chassis.

The milestones split into four **tier bands**, and world books gate
content against them:

| Tier | Milestones | What it means in play |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | 1–5 | Local. Your problems fit in a district, a village, a job. |
| **II** | 6–10 | Regional. Factions know your name; your problems have maps. |
| **III** | 11–15 | Pivotal. You bend the setting's big conflicts; they bend back. |
| **IV** | 16–20 | Defining. The world book's endgame — its towers, thrones, and final doors. |

A world book may lock anything to a tier — a district you can't survive, a
power that won't take a Tier-I student, a door that reads the ledger before
it opens. Tiers are the world's honest way of saying *not yet*, and the
ledger is the only way to change its mind. A world book's tier gates read
**milestones**, never display levels — a tower can't fake a Tier-IV door
open by inflating its level numbers.
