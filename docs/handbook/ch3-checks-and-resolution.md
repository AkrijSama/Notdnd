# Chapter 3 — Checks & Resolution

*The Inkborne System — Core Book*

Everything uncertain in Inkborne resolves the same way: **d20 + stat modifier
(+ proficiency, if trained) against a Difficulty (DC)**. This chapter is the
law of when that roll happens, what its outcomes are, and who gets to add
what. It is three laws long.

---

## LAW 1 — When dice happen

Every action a player declares falls into exactly one of three tiers. The
engine sorts it; the player never has to ask permission to act.

### Tier 0 — AUTOMATIC (no stakes, no roll)

**If failure has no cost, there is no roll.** The GM describes what *is*, and
the story continues. Automatic actions include:

- **Observation.** Looking around, noticing who's present, reading the mood
  of a room, recalling what your character plainly knows. *Perceiving your
  immediate surroundings is never a roll.* Ask "what do I see?" and the
  answer is the truth of the scene — every time, at no cost.
- **Safe conversation.** Talking with anyone who isn't set against you.
  Questions, banter, negotiation before it turns adversarial, asking an ally
  for help. *Speaking to a non-hostile character is never a roll.*
- **Trivial acts.** Anything a competent person of your stats simply does:
  climbing a ladder, lighting a fire in dry weather, buying goods at a fair
  price, walking somewhere you're allowed to be.

Two of these are worth stating as hard law, because other systems get them
wrong: **you never roll to look around, and you never roll to talk to someone
who means you no harm.** Dice that can fail imply a cost for failing; there
is no cost to having eyes or a voice.

### Tier 1 — CHECK (stakes exist, failure has a cost)

The moment failure would cost something — time, safety, secrecy, standing,
resources — the action becomes a **check**:

> **d20 + stat modifier + proficiency (if trained) vs DC**

The engine picks the stat that matches the attempt: STR to force the door,
DEX to cross the ledge, INT to reconstruct the cipher, Spirit to hold your
nerve against the thing in the dark. One roll, one comparison, and the
outcome lands in one of the three bands of Law 2.

Searching a stall for a hidden compartment is a check (you might miss it, and
the stall's owner might notice you looking). Glancing at the stall is not.
The line is always the same: *what does failing cost?* If the answer is
"nothing," it was Tier 0 all along.

### Tier 2 — CONTESTED (an opposing agent)

When the resistance is a *someone* rather than a *something*, both sides
roll: **your d20 + modifiers vs their d20 + modifiers**, highest total wins.
Sneaking past a sentry, arm-wrestling a dockhand, lying to an interrogator,
snatching the case before the other courier does. On a tie, the defender —
the side trying to keep things as they are — holds.

Contested rolls still resolve into the three bands: win clearly and you
succeed; lose narrowly and you may get what you wanted *at a cost*; lose
clearly and the opponent gains ground.

## LAW 2 — Three bands, never nothing

Every check resolves into exactly one of three outcomes. **There is no
outcome in which nothing happens.** Every band commits real state to the
world — this is by design, and it is what the story engine and the narrator
are built to consume: every roll hands them a *change*, never a shrug.

### ■ SUCCESS — meet or beat the DC

You get what you wanted, clean. The world updates: the lock is open, the
guard believes you, the ledge is behind you.

### ■ SUCCESS AT A COST — miss the DC by 1–4 **[PROPOSED]**

You get it — *and* a real cost commits alongside it. The lock opens, but it
took long enough that footsteps are on the stairs. The guard waves you
through, but he'll remember your face. The cost is always one of:

- **Time** — it took longer, and the world moved while you worked;
- **Resource** — it cost equipment, coin, or your own vitality;
- **Exposure** — someone noticed, something now knows;
- **Complication** — the situation grew a new problem that is now true.

The cost is not flavor text. It is committed state: the noticing NPC exists,
the spent resource is gone, the clock has actually advanced.

> **[PROPOSED — band boundary: within 4 of the DC.]** Rationale: on a d20,
> a 4-point window converts a flat 20% of all possible rolls into the middle
> band, independent of DC. Against a standard DC where a character needs an
> 11+, outcomes split roughly 50% success / 20% at-cost / 30% failure — a
> majority-forward distribution in which nearly three rolls in four move the
> player *toward* what they wanted, while still pricing in tension. A
> 2-point window (10%) makes the middle band too rare to feel like a pillar
> of the system; a 5-point window (25%) makes clean success feel unearned.
> Four is also mentally instant at the table: "close counts, within four."

### ■ FAILURE WITH CONSEQUENCE — miss by 5 or more

You don't get it, **and the situation changes**. Never "the lock stays
locked, try again." Failure moves the world: the pick snaps in the
mechanism, the guard's suspicion hardens into a challenge, the ice you were
crossing starts to crack. A failed check always leaves the scene *different*
— usually harder along the path you tried, and often with a new pressure
that wasn't there before.

This is the band that kills retry-spam without any retry rule: you *can*
try the lock again, but you're now trying a lock with a broken pick in it
while footsteps close in. The fiction did the enforcement.

**Critical rolls:** a natural 20 on a check is a clean success even against a
miss-by-margin, and a natural 1 is always failure-with-consequence
**[PROPOSED** — checks keep the d20's edge-drama, but *crit damage* remains
a combat-only concept**]**.

## LAW 3 — Training, not skills

There is **no skill list** in the Inkborne System. There are six stats, and
there is **training**.

- **Proficiency** is a single bonus that grows with your character
  (**+2 at the start, rising to +6 at the height of play**). You add it to a
  check when — and only when — you are **trained in a relevant domain**.
- **Domains** are areas of practiced competence: *streetcraft*, *fieldcraft*,
  *the blade*, *dead languages*, *engine-lore* — whatever the world you're
  playing in says its people can master. **Domains are defined by world
  books** and **granted by your background, class, and feats**. The core
  system doesn't hardcode a single one; it only defines what being trained
  *does*.
- **Relevance is judged, not looked up.** If your attempt plausibly draws on
  a domain you hold, proficiency applies. A courier trained in *streetcraft*
  adds it to reading a crowd, finding a fence, or spotting a tail — no table
  required.

Untrained is not helpless: you always roll your full stat. Training is the
difference between talent and craft.

### Difficulty ladder

| Difficulty | DC | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Easy** | **8** | A competent attempt usually clears it; rolled only because failure still costs something. |
| **Standard** | **12** | Honest work under pressure. The baseline of adventuring life. |
| **Hard** | **16** | Specialists shine here; the untrained need the die's favor. |
| **Heroic** | **20** | The stories other characters tell later. Trained, gifted, *and* lucky. |

The GM never announces a number mid-fiction — you learn the difficulty the
way your character would: the lock looks cheap, or it looks like it was
built by someone who'd met thieves before. *(The ladder sits 2 below the
numbers veterans of other d20 games may expect — deliberately, because in
this system missing-by-a-little still pays out. The middle band does the
work that inflated DCs used to.)* **[PROPOSED — ladder values]**

### Edge and burden

Circumstance bends the die before it bends the math:

- **Edge** — when the situation genuinely favors you (the right tool, the
  high ground, a distracted mark): **roll two d20s, keep the higher.**
- **Burden** — when it genuinely works against you (darkness, injury,
  improvised gear): **roll two d20s, keep the lower.**

Edge and burden don't stack — you either have edge, have burden, or roll
straight; if you'd have both, they cancel to a straight roll. They replace
piles of situational pluses and minuses: the fiction sets your footing, the
die does the rest.

---

*Law 1 decides whether dice touch the table. Law 2 guarantees that when they
do, the story moves. Law 3 decides who's better at what — without a skill
list. Combat runs on these same three laws, plus a clock: the turn queue,
covered next.*
