# Chapter 1 — How This Plays

*The Inkborne System — Core Book*

---

## What this is

Inkborne is a story game with a spine. You play by saying what your character
does — in your own words, at your own pace — and the world answers. Behind
every answer sits a real ruleset: a twenty-sided die, six stats, a turn queue,
and a referee that cannot be sweet-talked.

That last part is the point. In Inkborne, the storyteller and the judge are
two different things. The **Game Master narrates** — voice, texture, the smell
of rain on hot neon or cold stone. The **engine adjudicates** — whether the
lock opens, whether the blade lands, whether the lie holds. The narrator never
overrules the dice, and the dice never write the prose. You can charm an NPC.
You cannot charm the world out of its own rules.

Here is the whole system in one breath:

- **One die.** Everything uncertain resolves on a d20. Roll, add a stat,
  compare to a difficulty. You will learn this in your first minute of play
  and never need to relearn it.
- **Six stats.** Strength, Dexterity, Vitality, Spirit, Intellect, Luck.
  That's the entire sheet's foundation — everything else derives from these.
- **A turn queue, not turn order.** In combat, fast characters genuinely act
  more often. Speed isn't a tie-breaker rolled once at the start of a fight —
  it's tempo, all fight long, and you can see the queue coming.
- **Three outcomes, never zero.** Every roll ends in *success*, *success at a
  cost*, or *failure with consequence*. There is no "nothing happens." The
  story moves on every throw of the die.
- **A referee that keeps state.** What the world learns, it remembers. Doors
  you broke stay broken. Names you burned stay burned.

## The six stats

| Stat | What it is |
|---|---|
| **STR** — Strength | Raw force: what you can break, lift, and hit hard. |
| **DEX** — Dexterity | Speed and precision — and in combat, how *often* you act. |
| **VIT** — Vitality | Endurance and life: how much the world can take out of you. |
| **Spirit** | Will, presence, and attunement — the stat the unseen answers to. |
| **INT** — Intellect | Reasoning, recall, and craft: what you know and how fast you connect it. |
| **Luck** | The house edge. The stat for when the difference between outcomes is a coin toss. |

Your fighting numbers — Attack, Evasion, Accuracy, turn speed, critical
chance — are all **derived**. They compute from these six. There is no second
sheet of combat stats to manage; raise the source, and everything downstream
rises with it.

## What you never have to do

Inkborne deletes the busywork other systems make you carry:

- **No skill list to memorize.** There is no menu of thirty named skills to
  allocate and forget. You have six stats, and you're *trained* in the
  domains your background, class, and feats say you are. Trained means better
  at it. That's the whole rule; Chapter 3 gives you the numbers.
- **No rolling to look around.** Perceiving the scene in front of you is
  never a roll. Ask what you see, and the GM tells you what *is* — always.
  Searching a room for a hidden thing under time pressure is a roll. Having
  eyes is not.
- **No rolling to talk.** Conversation with someone who isn't set against you
  just *happens*. You speak, they answer, the scene breathes. Dice enter a
  conversation only when you're trying to move someone who has a reason to
  resist.
- **No rules lawyer duty.** The engine tracks the numbers, the queue, the
  difficulty, the consequences. Your job is to decide what your character
  does. The system's job is everything after that.

The short version of the design philosophy: **dice are for stakes.** If
failure costs nothing, there is no roll — the thing simply happens and the
story keeps moving. When the dice do come out, it's because something real is
on the table, and whatever they say, something real will change.

## One chassis, many worlds

This book is the **chassis**: the die, the stats, the checks, the turn queue,
the laws of resolution. You learn it once.

Everything with a *setting's* fingerprints on it — races, classes, powers,
gear, bestiaries, laws, lore — lives in **world books**. A world book plugs
into this chassis and tells it what a person can be there: what races walk
that world and the feat each one carries, what a "sword" or a "sidearm" or a
"soulbrand" actually is, what training domains exist, what powers can be
learned and what they cost.

The contract runs one way. The core book never mentions a world; a world book
never rewrites a law of the core. So when you cross from a neon sprawl to a
tower that eats climbers to a wasteland with your third character, your hands
already know the game. Only the world is new.

That's the promise of this chapter, and the rest of the core book keeps it:
**learn the system once, then spend the rest of your time playing worlds.**
